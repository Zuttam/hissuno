## Week 1 Posts

### Post L1: "AI coding tools speed up the wrong part" (Pillar 1)

**Format:** Long-form text (6 paragraphs)
**Recommended to post first** - strongest hook, broadest appeal

```
AI coding tools speed up the part that was already fast.

Writing code was never the bottleneck. Not in any team I've worked with, not in any codebase I've inherited, not in any startup I've built.

The bottleneck was always upstream. Understanding what to build. Figuring out which problem actually matters. Translating a vague customer complaint into something an engineer can act on.

We've automated the output side of engineering. Code generation, autocomplete, test scaffolding. And it's impressive. I use these tools every day and they genuinely make me faster at producing code.

But producing code was never the hard part. The hard part was producing the right code. And that requires understanding context that no model has access to - the customer who called support three times this week, the workaround that sales promised, the edge case that only shows up in production.

We poured billions into making engineers type faster. The gap between what customers need and what gets built is exactly the same size it was five years ago.

#AI #Engineering #ProductDevelopment
```

### Post L2: "Customer feedback decays at every handoff" (Pillar 2)

**Format:** Long-form text (7 paragraphs)

```
Customer feedback doesn't disappear. It decays.

Every handoff strips away context. Support hears the frustration, the workaround, the "I've been dealing with this for months." They log a ticket. Already half the signal is gone.

PM reads the ticket. They see a feature request where there was actually a broken workflow. They add it to the backlog with a priority label. More signal lost.

Engineering picks it up three sprints later. By now it's a one-line description of something nobody fully understood in the first place. The engineer builds what the ticket says, not what the customer needed.

Ship it. Close the ticket. Customer still has the same problem.

I've seen this pattern in every company I've worked at. The people building the product are the furthest from the people using it. And every layer in between is a lossy compression algorithm.

The fix isn't better ticketing systems. It's shorter distance between the person with the problem and the person building the solution.

#ProductManagement #Engineering #CustomerFeedback
```

### Post L4: "Feature factories and broken signal chains" (Pillar 2)

**Format:** Long-form text (6 paragraphs)

```
Most feature factories aren't caused by bad prioritization. They're caused by a broken signal chain.

Here's what happens. The loudest account dictates the roadmap. Not because their need is the most common. Because their voice is the only one that reaches engineering.

Meanwhile, dozens of customers with the same problem never escalate. They churn quietly, or they build workarounds, or they just stop using that part of the product.

The PM sees a backlog full of one-off requests. The engineer sees a sprint full of disconnected features. Nobody sees the pattern, because the pattern only exists in aggregate across hundreds of conversations nobody is reading.

Feature factories are a symptom. The disease is that the signal from customers to builders is routed through too many intermediaries, each with their own filters, priorities, and interpretations.

Fix the signal chain and the roadmap fixes itself. Every team I've seen break out of feature factory mode did it the same way - they got engineers closer to customers. Not through dashboards. Through actual exposure to real conversations.

#ProductManagement #Engineering #Startups
```

### Post L5: "The new engineering craft: writing constraints" (Pillar 1)

**Format:** Long-form text (5 paragraphs)

```
The new engineering skill isn't writing code. It's writing constraints.

Engineers have always adapted. From managing memory to managing cloud infrastructure. From writing assembly to writing React. Each shift moved us up a level of abstraction.

AI is the next shift. And the skill that matters most isn't knowing how to prompt a model. It's knowing how to constrain one. Defining what the system should NOT do is harder than defining what it should do. Every production AI system I've built has more guardrails than features.

Telling a machine what not to do is harder than doing it yourself. That's the paradox. The more capable the tool, the more important the boundaries. An AI that can generate anything will generate the wrong thing unless you've built walls around its decision space.

This is engineering craft in 2026. Not syntax. Not frameworks. Constraint design. The engineers who thrive will be the ones who can think in boundaries, not just in outputs.

#AI #Engineering #SoftwareDevelopment
```

### Post L6: "The backlog graveyard" (Pillar 2)

**Format:** Long-form text (6 paragraphs)
**LinkedIn original** - not adapted from X

```
Your backlog is a graveyard of customer requests that nobody triaged.

I've audited backlogs at companies of every size. Same pattern everywhere. Hundreds of tickets from the last 12 months. Most untouched. Many duplicates that nobody connected. A few tagged "important" by someone who left the company.

The backlog isn't a planning tool. It's a guilt repository. A place where requests go to make everyone feel like they were heard, while nothing actually happens.

Here's what makes it worse. The customers who generated those requests have moved on. They've churned, or found a workaround, or stopped caring. The requests are stale. But they're still sitting in the queue, competing with today's actual priorities for attention.

The best product teams I've seen don't maintain backlogs. They maintain live signal. They know what customers are struggling with right now, not what someone logged eight months ago.

Kill the backlog. Replace it with a system that surfaces recurring patterns from actual conversations. The customers will tell you what matters. You just need to listen in real time, not on a six-month delay.

#ProductManagement #Startups #Engineering
```

```
5 things AI changed in engineering.
And 5 it absolutely didn't.

```

```
You still spend most of your time figuring out
```

```
Nobody writes CRUD endpoints from scratch anymore.
The tedious 80% writes itself.
```

Slide 5:
```
DIDN'T CHANGE: Debugging production issues.

AI can write code.
It can't explain why your service crashed at 3 AM
with a customer-specific edge case.
```

Slide 6:
```
CHANGED: Who can build software.

The barrier to entry dropped.
More people can create.
That's genuinely good.
```

Slide 7:
```
DIDN'T CHANGE: Who can build software well.

Taste, architecture, tradeoff thinking.
These still take years to develop.
AI didn't shortcut experience.
```

Slide 8:
```
CHANGED: Code review volume.

There's simply more code to review.
AI generates faster than humans can verify.
```

Slide 9:
```
DIDN'T CHANGE: Team communication.

The standup is still awkward.
The requirements doc is still unclear.
The PM and engineer still interpret the same sentence differently.
```

```
AI changed the tools.
It didn't change the game.

The game is still:
understand the problem, build the right thing,
ship it to someone who cares.
```
